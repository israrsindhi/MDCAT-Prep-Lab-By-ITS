Logical Reasoning, the high-yield way

These are the topics that show up on MDCAT test after test — condensed into exam-ready notes, not a full textbook rewrite. Use them to revise fast between mock tests, then go back to your textbook for anything that still feels shaky.

Analogies & Classification High-yield

  • Analogies test the *relationship* between a pair of words/items, not the words themselves — first name the relationship explicitly (e.g. 'part-to-whole', 'tool-to-user', 'cause-to-effect') before matching it to the answer options.
  • Common analogy relationship types: synonym/antonym, part-whole, worker-tool, cause-effect, category-member, degree of intensity.
  • Classification (odd-one-out) questions require identifying the shared property of the majority first, then spotting which single item breaks that pattern — don't just look for the 'odd-looking' item.
  • Watch for classification traps based on superficial similarity (e.g. spelling pattern) rather than true category membership (e.g. meaning or function).

Series Completion & Coding-Decoding High-yield

  • Number series: check for a constant difference (arithmetic), constant ratio (geometric), alternating pattern, or a squares/cubes-based pattern before guessing.
  • Letter series: convert letters to their numeric positions (A=1...Z=26) when a pattern isn't obvious visually — this often reveals a hidden numeric series.
  • Coding-decoding by letter shift: identify a fixed forward/backward shift applied to each letter of the original word (e.g. each letter shifted +2 in the alphabet).
  • Coding-decoding by substitution: some questions use symbol-for-letter or number-for-letter substitution codes — build the mapping systematically from the given example before applying it to the target word.
  • Always verify your derived rule against every given letter/number pair in the example, not just the first one — a rule that fits only part of the pattern will lead to a wrong answer.

Blood Relations & Direction Sense High-yield

  • Draw a quick family tree diagram as you read blood-relation questions — don't try to hold the relationships in your head, since MDCAT relation chains are often 3+ steps long.
  • Key relation logic: 'mother's brother' = maternal uncle; 'father's sister's husband' = uncle (by marriage); 'son of my father's son' = my nephew or myself, depending on context — resolve gender/identity carefully.
  • Direction sense questions are solved by sketching a simple compass grid and plotting each movement step by step, tracking net displacement rather than every turn.
  • For 'shortest distance' questions after multiple turns, use the Pythagorean theorem on the net horizontal and vertical displacement rather than adding up the path length.

Syllogisms & Statement-Based Reasoning High-yield

  • A syllogism's conclusion must follow strictly from the given premises alone — never bring in outside/real-world knowledge, even if a premise seems factually false.
  • Use Venn diagrams for 'All/Some/No' statements: 'All A are B' draws A entirely inside B; 'Some A are B' draws overlapping circles; 'No A are B' draws separate circles.
  • A conclusion is valid only if it holds true for every possible diagram consistent with the premises — if even one valid diagram contradicts the conclusion, it does not follow.
  • Statement–assumption questions require distinguishing an assumption (an unstated premise the argument depends on) from an inference (a conclusion that follows from what's stated) — they are commonly confused.
  • Statement–conclusion questions ask what *must* be true given the statement, not what is merely plausible or likely.

Arithmetical Reasoning & Data Sufficiency High-yield

  • Arithmetical reasoning MCQs are typically short word problems on ratio, percentage, average, speed-distance-time, or simple profit/loss — set up one equation from the problem before attempting mental shortcuts.
  • For data sufficiency-style questions, check whether each given statement alone is enough to answer the question, then check both together — don't solve the full problem unless the question demands the actual numeric answer.
  • Percentage-to-fraction shortcuts (e.g. 25% = 1/4, 12.5% = 1/8) save significant time under exam pressure — memorise the common ones.
  • For average-based questions, remember: total = average × number of items — most 'average' MCQs reduce to this single relationship.
