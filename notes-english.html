English, the high-yield way

These are the topics that show up on MDCAT test after test — condensed into exam-ready notes, not a full textbook rewrite. Use them to revise fast between mock tests, then go back to your textbook for anything that still feels shaky.

Tenses High-yield

  • English has 12 main tense forms across past/present/future, each split into simple, continuous, perfect, and perfect continuous — know the structural pattern (auxiliary + verb form) for each, not just the name.
  • Present simple is used for habitual actions and general truths ('Water boils at 100°C'); present continuous for actions happening right now or fixed future plans.
  • Present perfect (have/has + past participle) links a past action to the present ('I have finished') — MDCAT frequently tests confusion between past simple and present perfect.
  • Past perfect (had + past participle) describes an action completed before another past action — essential for narration/reported speech questions.
  • Subject–verb agreement: singular subjects take singular verbs even with intervening phrases ('Each of the students has...', not 'have').
  • Sequence of tenses: if the main clause is in the past, the subordinate clause generally also shifts to a past form, except for universal truths.
  • Watch for irregular past participles (gone/went, done/did, seen/saw) — a classic single-word error-spotting trap.

Active & Passive Voice High-yield

  • Active voice: subject performs the action ('The doctor examined the patient'); passive voice: subject receives the action ('The patient was examined by the doctor').
  • Passive formation: object of active sentence → subject of passive; verb becomes a form of 'be' + past participle; original subject becomes the 'by' agent (often omittable).
  • Only transitive verbs (verbs that take a direct object) can be converted to passive voice — intransitive verbs (e.g. 'arrive', 'sleep') cannot.
  • The tense of the active sentence determines the form of 'be' used in the passive (is/was/has been/will be, etc.) — match tense carefully during conversion.
  • Passive voice is preferred when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context — common in scientific/formal writing, which MDCAT English draws heavily from.
  • Modal verbs form passive as modal + be + past participle ('The work must be completed').

Narration (Direct & Indirect Speech) High-yield

  • Reporting verb changes: 'says/say' stays the same if reporting present speech, but 'said' triggers a full tense backshift in the reported clause.
  • Tense backshift: present simple → past simple, present continuous → past continuous, present perfect → past perfect, will → would, can → could, may → might.
  • Pronoun changes follow logic, not a fixed rule: shift pronouns to match who is speaking and who is being spoken about in the new context.
  • Time and place word changes: now → then, today → that day, tomorrow → the next day, here → there, this → that.
  • Interrogative sentences in indirect speech use reporting verbs like 'asked' and drop the question mark and auxiliary inversion ('He asked if/whether...' or 'He asked what...').
  • Imperative sentences use 'told/ordered/requested + to-infinitive' ('He told me to leave'), and exclamatory sentences typically become statements introduced by 'that', capturing the emotion in words rather than punctuation.

Articles, Prepositions & Conjunctions High-yield

  • 'A' before consonant sounds, 'an' before vowel sounds — based on pronunciation, not spelling ('an hour', but 'a university').
  • 'The' is used for specific/previously mentioned nouns, superlatives, unique objects (the sun), and some geographic names (rivers, oceans, mountain ranges).
  • Zero article is used with plural/uncountable nouns in a general sense, most proper nouns, and languages/subjects.
  • Common fixed preposition pairs are a high-yield rote-memory area: depend ON, interested IN, married TO, good AT, afraid OF, capable OF, similar TO.
  • Prepositions of time: 'at' for precise times, 'on' for days/dates, 'in' for months/years/longer periods.
  • Correlative conjunctions must match in structure (parallelism): either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, both...and.
  • Subordinating conjunctions (although, because, since, unless) introduce dependent clauses; coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) join independent clauses.

Vocabulary — Synonyms, Antonyms & Idioms High-yield

  • Learn vocabulary in word families/roots rather than isolated words — recognising a common Latin/Greek root (e.g. 'bene-' = good, 'mal-' = bad) lets you infer meaning under exam pressure.
  • For synonym/antonym MCQs, first identify the part of speech and the word's likely context/tone (positive, negative, neutral) before matching options — this eliminates half the choices quickly.
  • Build a running list of idioms and one-word substitutions from past papers rather than a generic dictionary list — MDCAT tends to reuse a recognisable pool of items.
  • Watch for 'false friend' options: words that look similar to the correct answer but have a different or opposite meaning — a very common distractor technique.
  • Practising with short daily vocabulary sets (10–15 words) retains far better than cramming long lists once.

Sentence Correction & Error Detection High-yield

  • Subject–verb agreement errors are the single most common error type — check the true grammatical subject, not the noun physically closest to the verb.
  • Parallelism errors occur when list items or compared items don't share the same grammatical form ('I like reading, writing, and to swim' is wrong — should be 'and swimming').
  • Redundancy: eliminate phrases that repeat meaning unnecessarily ('return back', 'the reason is because').
  • Misplaced/dangling modifiers: a descriptive phrase must sit next to the word it actually modifies, or the sentence implies something illogical.
  • Read the whole sentence first before selecting an underlined correction — errors are sometimes only obvious in relation to the rest of the sentence.
  • When multiple options look grammatically valid, choose the most concise, standard-English option — MDCAT favours clarity over stylistic flourish.

Reading Comprehension High-yield

  • Skim the passage first for overall structure and main idea before reading questions in detail — don't try to memorise every fact on the first pass.
  • Read the questions before re-reading the passage in full, so you know exactly what to look for.
  • Answer strictly from the passage's content and logic — never rely on outside general knowledge, even if you know more about the topic.
  • For 'main idea' questions, the answer usually reflects the passage as a whole, not just one paragraph or a minor detail.
  • For inference questions, the correct answer is strongly implied but not directly stated — eliminate options that are either too literal (directly quoted, but for a different question type) or too big a leap beyond the text.
  • Manage time strictly: with English carrying only a small share of total marks, don't over-invest time on any single comprehension passage at the expense of other subjects.
